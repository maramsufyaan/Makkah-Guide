    Hajj to the Sacred House of God is one of the pillars of Islam, according to God Almighty’s saying, “And God has responsibility over the people to perform Hajj to the House, whoever is able to make a way to it,” and his saying, may God’s prayers and peace be upon him: “Islam is built on five things: testifying that there is no god but God and that Muhammad is the Messenger of God, establishing prayer, paying zakat, and fasting during Ramadan.” And the Hajj to the House is performed by whoever is able to make it.) It was made obligatory in the ninth year of the Hijra.
    Outlined in Islamic teachings, certain conditions must be met for an individual to fulfill their obligation of performing Hajj:
    • Islam since a non-Muslim is not obligated to perform Hajj, and if he performs it, it is not valid
    • The obligation which is that the Muslim must be an adult and sane
    • Freedom as it is not obligatory for an owned slave to perform Hajj because he is unable, but if he performs Hajj it is valid on his part, and he is required to perform Hajj after his freedom.
    • Ability which is in terms of money and body, such that he has money with which he can perform Hajj, and he is also physically healthy and not incapable of performing the rituals. If the person obligated is unable, neither physically nor financially, then in this case Hajj is not obligatory for him, because the condition of ability is not met.
    • The presence of a mahram for the woman Hajj, the pilgrimage to the Sacred House of God in Mecca, is a fundamental pillar of Islam, mandated by God in the Quran and emphasized by the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him). It represents a significant spiritual journey for Muslims worldwide, symbolizing unity, devotion, and submission to the will of God.

    Hajj has four pillars:

    • Ihram: The state of consecration entered into before performing Hajj rituals.
    • Standing at Arafat: The essential act of standing at the plain of Arafat until sunset for those who stand during the day.
    • Tawaf al-Ifadah: The ritual of circumambulating the Kaaba after returning from Arafat.
    • Sa'i: The act of walking between the hills of Safa and Marwah.
    As for the obligatory acts of Hajj, there are seven:
    • Assuming the state of Ihram at the appointed time.
    • Standing at Arafat until sunset for those who stand during the day.
    • .Spending the night at Muzdalifah until after midnight
    • Spending the night at Mina.
    • Systematically stoning the pillar during the nights of the days of Tashreeq.
    • Shaving or shortening the hair.
    • Performing the farewell Tawaf (Tawaf al-Wada') if the pilgrim is not a resident of Mecca or its vicinity. Additionally, the pilgrim must offer a sacrifice (Qurban).
    • As for the rest of the actions and sayings of Hajj, they are Sunnahs (recommended practices), such as the Tawaf upon arrival, spending the night at Mina on the night of Arafat, walking between Safa and Marwah, counting the pebbles, throwing them at the Jamrah, receiving applications, and ascending from Safa to Marwah.
    The difference between a pillar, an obligation, and a Sunnah is that a pillar is indispensable for the validity of Hajj, while the obligation can be skipped, though one must offer a sacrifice if skipped. As for the Sunnah, there is no obligation if left unperformed.

    Why are there special sites (Miqats) for entering into Ihram?

    Out of respect for the Honored House of Allah and the rituals of Hajj and Umrah, the Messenger of Allah (blessings and peace be upon him) specified places before reaching Makkah, where pilgrims enter into the state of Ihram, and from which they begin reciting their Talbiyah before reaching the Haram. After specifying the exact locations of these places, the Prophet (blessings and peace be upon him) said: "These Miqats are for the people at those very places and those who come through those places with the intention of performing Hajj and Umrah". These sites differ in their distance from and proximity to the Grand Mosque, depending on which direction you come from. Pilgrims who live beyond those Miqats are obliged to enter into Ihram at the Miqats or any adjacent or parallel points on the way, whether they travel by air or by land. They should not go beyond them without entering the state of Ihram.

    Determining your Miqats The timing of Hajj is both temporal and spatial. Temporal: It refers to the specific months designated for Hajj, which are Shawwal, Dhul-Qi'dah, and Dhul-Hijjah, specifically from the first of Shawwal to the tenth of Dhul-Hijjah. Spatial: As outlined by Muhammad (peace be upon him), the spatial aspects of Hajj are as follows:

    • Dhul-Hulaifa: the Miqat for the people of Madina. It is 420 km from Makkah.
    • Al-Juhfah: the Miqat for the people of the Levant and those on their way. It is 187 km from Makkah. Today, people enter Ihram in Rabigh, which is 204 km from Makkah.
    • Qarn Al-Manazil: the Miqat for the people of Najd and those on their way. It is 94 km away from Makkah.
    • Yalamlam: the Miqat for the people of Yemen and those on their way. It is 115 km away from Makkah.
    • Dhat Irq: the Miqat for the people of Iraq and those on their way. It is 92 km away from Makkah.

    types of rituals

    Al-Tamattu' (Enjoyment): This involves performing Umrah alone from the Miqat during the months of Hajj, proclaiming the intention for Ihram by saying "Labbayka 'Umrah."
    • Then, the pilgrim proceeds to perform the rituals of Umrah, including Tawaf, Sa'i, and either shaving the head or trimming the hair.
    • This allows the pilgrim to remove all restrictions of Ihram until the eighth day of Dhu al-Hijjah, known as the Day of Tarwiyah
    • On the eighth day, the pilgrim enters into Ihram for Hajj alone and completes all Hajj rituals.

    Al-Qiran (Combining): This involves consecrating Ihram for both Umrah and Hajj together, by saying "Labbayka 'Umrah wa Hajjan," or consecrating Ihram for Umrah from the Miqat and then consecrating Ihram for Hajj before beginning Tawa

    • Upon reaching Mecca, the pilgrim performs the Tawaf of Arrival.
    • If they wish to advance Sa'i for Hajj, they do so between Safa and Marwah
    • otherwise, they delay it until after Tawaf al-Ifadah. They neither shave nor trim their hair nor exit Ihram until the Day of Sacrifice.

    Al-Ifrad (Single): This involves consecrating Ihram for Hajj alone by saying "Labbayka Hajjan."
    • Upon reaching Mecca, the pilgrim performs the Tawaf of Arrival and may perform Sa'i for Hajj if desired.
    • Otherwise, they postpone it until after Tawaf al-Ifadah, similar to Qiran.

      • they remain in Ihram until they exit it on the day of Eid al-Adha.


    The actions of the pilgrim in Al-Ifrad and Al-Qiran are similar. However, in the case of Al-Qiran, offering a sacrifice (Hadi) is obligatory due to the combination of two rituals, unlike in Al-Ifrad, where it is not obligatory as only one ritual, Hajj, is performed.
